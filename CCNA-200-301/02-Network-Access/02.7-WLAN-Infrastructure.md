# WLAN Infrastructure

## Definition
WLAN infrastructure includes the APs, controllers, switches, VLANs, trunks, DHCP, DNS, authentication, and RF design needed for wireless clients to connect.

## Simple explanation
Wi-Fi is not just the AP. Wireless clients depend on the wired network and services behind the AP.

## Core components
- Wireless clients
- Access points
- Wireless LAN controller or cloud management
- Access switches and trunks
- VLANs and default gateways
- DHCP and DNS
- Authentication servers such as RADIUS

## WLAN mapping
A WLAN/SSID is usually mapped to a VLAN. For example, `CORP-WIFI` may map to VLAN 20, while `GUEST-WIFI` maps to VLAN 30.

## Important design areas
- Coverage: signal reaches the required area.
- Capacity: enough APs and channels for client load.
- Security: WPA2/WPA3, 802.1X, guest isolation.
- Roaming: clients can move between APs smoothly.
- Wired readiness: trunks, VLANs, DHCP, and gateways are correct.

## Troubleshooting checks
- Is the SSID enabled and being advertised?
- Can the AP reach the controller?
- Is the client authenticating successfully?
- Is the correct VLAN assigned?
- Is DHCP working for wireless clients?
- Are signal strength and interference acceptable?

## Exam focus
A WLAN often maps an SSID to a VLAN. Wireless problems can come from RF, security, DHCP, VLAN, trunk, or routing issues.

## Quick review
WLAN infrastructure combines wireless radios with wired switching, VLANs, services, and security.
