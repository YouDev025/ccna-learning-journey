# Firewalls and IPS

## Definition
A firewall controls traffic based on security policy. An IPS, or Intrusion Prevention System, inspects traffic for malicious behavior and can block attacks.

## Simple explanation
A firewall is like a security checkpoint. An IPS is like an inspector that looks for suspicious patterns inside allowed traffic.

## Firewall functions
- Permit or deny traffic based on source, destination, ports, protocols, or applications.
- Track sessions with stateful inspection.
- Segment networks into security zones.
- Support NAT and VPN services on many platforms.
- Log events for monitoring and troubleshooting.

## IPS functions
- Detect known attack signatures.
- Identify abnormal or suspicious behavior.
- Drop, reset, or alert on malicious traffic.
- Help protect servers, users, and network infrastructure.

## Firewall vs IPS
| Feature | Firewall | IPS |
|---|---|---|
| Primary purpose | Enforce access policy | Stop attacks |
| Common decision basis | IP, port, protocol, app, zone | Signatures, behavior, reputation |
| Action | Permit, deny, inspect, log | Alert, drop, reset, block |

## Placement examples
- Internet edge: protect the organization from outside traffic.
- Data center: protect important servers.
- Internal segmentation: separate departments or security zones.
- Branch edge: protect remote offices.

## Exam focus
- Firewalls are policy enforcement devices.
- IPS is inline and can actively block traffic.
- IDS is usually passive and alerts rather than blocks.
- Security devices can affect connectivity, so always consider policy during troubleshooting.

## Quick review
Firewalls decide what traffic is allowed; IPS tools look for attacks and can stop them.
