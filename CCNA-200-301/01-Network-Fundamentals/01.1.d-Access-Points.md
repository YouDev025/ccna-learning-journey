# Access Points

## Definition
A wireless access point, or AP, connects wireless clients to a wired network. APs bridge wireless 802.11 traffic to Ethernet networks.

## Simple explanation
An AP is the wireless door into the LAN. Laptops and phones connect to the AP, and the AP connects them to the rest of the network.

## Main jobs
- Advertise one or more wireless networks using SSIDs.
- Authenticate and associate wireless clients.
- Convert wireless frames to wired Ethernet frames.
- Support encryption such as WPA2 or WPA3.
- Use radio channels in bands such as 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz.

## Autonomous vs lightweight APs
| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Autonomous AP | Configured and managed individually |
| Lightweight AP | Managed by a wireless LAN controller |
| Cloud-managed AP | Managed from a vendor cloud dashboard |

## Important wireless terms
- SSID: wireless network name.
- BSSID: AP radio MAC address for an SSID.
- Channel: frequency range used for transmission.
- Roaming: client movement between APs.
- RF interference: signals that disrupt wireless communication.

## Example
A phone joins the SSID `Campus-WiFi`. The AP authenticates the client, bridges its traffic into the wired LAN, and sends the traffic toward the default gateway.

## Exam focus
- APs provide wireless access, not routing by default.
- Multiple APs can use the same SSID for roaming.
- Channel planning reduces interference.

## Quick review
Access points connect wireless clients to the wired network.
