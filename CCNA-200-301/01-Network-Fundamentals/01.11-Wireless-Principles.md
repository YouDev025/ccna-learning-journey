# Wireless Principles

## Definition
Wireless networking uses radio frequencies to connect clients without physical cables. Wi-Fi is based on IEEE 802.11 standards.

## Main wireless concepts
- SSID: the wireless network name.
- BSSID: the MAC address of an AP radio for an SSID.
- Channel: a slice of radio frequency used for communication.
- Band: frequency range such as 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, or 6 GHz.
- Roaming: a client moving from one AP to another.
- Association: client connection to an AP.

## 2.4 GHz vs 5 GHz
| Band | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|
| 2.4 GHz | Better range, better wall penetration | More interference, fewer non-overlapping channels |
| 5 GHz | More channels, usually faster | Shorter range than 2.4 GHz |
| 6 GHz | More clean spectrum for newer Wi-Fi | Requires newer client/AP support |

## Security
- Open networks provide no encryption.
- WPA2 is common and safer than older WEP/WPA.
- WPA3 improves security when supported.
- Enterprise wireless often uses 802.1X authentication.

## Interference sources
- Neighboring Wi-Fi networks
- Microwave ovens
- Bluetooth devices
- Wireless cameras
- Physical obstacles and reflective surfaces

## Design basics
- Avoid channel overlap.
- Place APs for coverage and capacity.
- Use appropriate transmit power.
- Separate guest and corporate access.
- Plan for roaming in areas with mobile users.

## Exam focus
Wireless is half-duplex shared media. Only one device transmits on a channel at a time, so interference and contention affect performance.

## Quick review
Wireless design depends on coverage, capacity, channels, security, and interference control.
